Historical context

The Baroni College, located at 123 Via Pignolo and today home to the University of Bergamo, was founded at the end of the nineteenth century as an elementary school with an attached boarding school, commissioned by Professor Angelo Baroni. It later became a boarding school of the Royal Technical Industrial Institute, hosting students from across the province.

During the German occupation, on 3 October 1943, the building was requisitioned and transformed into the headquarters of the German Gendarmerie Command and into a political prison.

Why it is a place of memory

Between 1943 and 1945, the Baroni College was one of the main sites of Nazi repression in Bergamo. Anti-fascists and partisans accused of crimes against German authority were imprisoned and interrogated here, before being transferred to the Sant‚ÄôAgata prison or to the monastery of Matris Domini.

Numerous testimonies recount extremely harsh interrogations and inhuman torture. Don Mario Benigni suffered violence for days, while other prisoners were brutally killed. This building stands as a symbol of the violence of the Nazi occupation and of the sacrifice of those who fought for freedom.

In-depth study: transfer of prisoners and testimonies

According to several testimonies, in January 1944 the SS guarding the Baroni College were recalled to the front and the building was emptied of prisoners during the night of the 21st. At least five witnesses ‚Äì Franco Maj, Cesare Bonino, Giovan Battista Cortinovis, Luigi Mondini, Giacomo Paganoni ‚Äì recount that night.

The prisoners, according to Cesare Bonino a total of 56 under the custody of about ten SS soldiers, were brought out into the courtyard under the watchful eyes of their worried guards, as noted by Cortinovis, and transferred to the Sant‚ÄôAgata prison, where a German garrison had been installed since December 1943.

This was not a definitive closure of the Baroni: even in March 1944, captured partisans were brought there, such as Adriana Locatelli and some of her companions from the Maresana group. It is likely that the very limited German staff was supported by fascist personnel.

The comparison of testimonies reveals an increasingly efficient organization of the fascist repressive apparatus, in which figures such as Resmini, Strohmenger, Monge, Ghisleni, Zanchi, Messaggi emerge, working in complete synergy with the Nazi one.
