The Special Tribunal was established by the fascist regime on November 25, 1926, and became operational in 1927. After an initial suppression in July 1943, it was re-established by the Italian Social Republic and remained active until the Liberation, with a branch also operating in Bergamo.

The stabilization of the 1930s

In the 1930s, the Special Tribunal of Bergamo played a central role in the stabilization of fascist justice, particularly thanks to the 1931 Extension Law.

Its powers expanded: the tribunal was no longer limited to repressing anti-fascism, but became more broadly involved in defending the personality of the State and the security of the regime.

Although it represented the judicial apex of political repression, the tribunal did not hold a monopoly in this field, but operated within a multi-centered system alongside the Ministry of the Interior, the police, the Carabinieri, and the Fascist Party, with frequent overlaps of authority. Within this system, the Special Tribunal of Bergamo played a selective role: it mainly judged the most serious and symbolically significant political crimes, while everyday surveillance and preventive control remained the responsibility of other bodies. In this way, it helped make political repression a stable and ordinary element of the fascist legal system, strengthening the consolidation of the regime.

Why it is a place of memory

The Special Tribunal represents one of the most repressive instruments of the fascist dictatorship. Created after the introduction of the ‚Äúfascist laws,‚Äù it was used to strike down any form of political opposition, eliminating fundamental freedoms such as freedom of the press, association, and the right to strike.

Trials were swift and devoid of guarantees: defendants often did not know the charges against them, had no right to appeal, and could even be sentenced to death. Verdicts were frequently predetermined, turning the tribunal into a powerful tool of fear and control.

The Special Tribunal reveals the true face of dictatorship: a state that used the law not to guarantee justice, but to suppress dissent.

Further insight: after the Liberation

After the fall of fascism and the end of World War II, places like this lost their repressive function. In the climate of democratic reconstruction of the Italian Republic, the building was repurposed for educational use, becoming the home of the Art High School founded in the early 1960s. Transforming a place associated with control and repression into a school reflected the desire to return these spaces to the community and to affirm new values based on education, culture, and freedom.

The subsequent naming of the school after Giacomo and Pio Manz√π reinforces the symbolic meaning of this transformation: from the headquarters of a tribunal that suppressed free thought to a place devoted to creativity and artistic expression. Today, studying in this building means inhabiting a space that tells the story of Italy‚Äôs transition from dictatorship to democracy, turning a place of repression into one of education and freedom.
